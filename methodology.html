HOW COMMUNITY RECORDS WORK

How community records are organised, reviewed, grouped, and kept evidence-safe.

RETURN TO NSW PORTALS

Simple rule

The portal helps residents create clear community records. It does not decide what is true or make findings on behalf of residents. Instead, it keeps resident accounts, evidence, observations, questions, and conclusions separate so each can be understood in its proper context.

Resident accounts

A resident account records what someone saw, experienced, heard, photographed, reported, or was told. Wherever possible, the resident's own words are preserved so the account remains an authentic record of their experience.

Evidence layers

Every community record is organised into separate evidence layers. Keeping these layers separate helps everyone understand what was reported, what is supported by evidence, what has been observed, and what still needs to be verified.

  • Resident account: the person's own words.
  • Evidence held: photos, videos, maps, letters, emails, dates, reference numbers, witnesses, reports, or other records.
  • Observation: what can be seen, heard, located, dated, or described.
  • Inference: what the information may suggest, clearly treated as not yet confirmed.
  • Verification needed: what needs inspection, testing, disclosure, confirmation, or professional review.
  • Action sought: what the resident wants an organisation, representative, reviewer, or community process to do.

Geospatial Canopy Health Protocol

This protocol is used where a local issue involves tree canopy condition, suspected dieback, repeated roost pressure, access corridors, environmental change, or visible canopy damage over time.

Google Earth, historical imagery, GPS-tagged ground photos, direction or compass-bearing photos, tilt or camera-angle notes, street-view comparisons, and near-infrared or vegetation-health imagery where available can be used together to build a time-sequenced canopy record.

  • Baseline: record the earliest available canopy condition and site context.
  • Repeat observation: compare later imagery against the baseline using the same tree, canopy group, or mapped location.
  • Ground control: attach phone photos that show GPS location, direction, tilt, date, and visible target canopy.
  • Canopy indicators: record crown thinning, leaf loss, branch dieback, canopy gaps, colour change, visible roost concentration, and damaged limbs.
  • Infrared or vegetation-health layer: where available, use near-infrared or vegetation index evidence to flag possible canopy stress earlier than normal visible imagery.
  • AI-assisted review: use AI to help compare imagery, locate repeated change zones, organise observations, and identify where follow-up photos should be taken.

Evidence limit: geospatial and canopy imagery can show location, pattern, proximity, visible change, and possible vegetation stress. It does not prove cause, responsibility, breach, disease, contamination, or the exact biological mechanism without further verification.

Discovery before conclusion

Community Discovery is designed for issues that are still emerging. It allows residents to document concerns before they become established community issues. A discovery record does not determine whether an issue is proven—it helps reveal whether independent resident accounts identify the same pattern over time.

How an issue becomes priority

A community issue becomes a higher priority when independent resident accounts consistently point to the same concern. Archive receipts help identify those shared patterns over time.

  • Volume: multiple people submit records about the same issue.
  • Convergence: accounts point to the same location, event, authority, process, or impact.
  • Severity: the concern affects safety, health, access, homes, children, elderly people, money, environment, or daily community life.
  • Evidence strength: records include photos, documents, official replies, dates, reference numbers, or witnesses.
  • Response gap: the record shows no reply, unclear reply, delay, contradiction, or missing information.
  • Fixability: there is a clear responsible body, clear action request, or clear document gap.
  • Community value: a dedicated section would help residents understand and act together.

Promotion model

Community discovery is open for local accounts. If enough accounts identify the same concern, that concern may become a dedicated public-facing section. The discovery space then continues for future issues.

Why these rules matter

These rules help protect the accuracy of community records. They reduce misunderstandings, preserve resident accounts, and ensure different types of evidence are not confused with one another.

  • Calling something proven before it is verified.
  • Turning a resident account into a third-person summary without permission.
  • Claiming a process has started when it has not.
  • Mixing map, location, material, health, canopy, geospatial, or response evidence together.
  • Letting separate resident accounts disappear without a shared record.
  • Letting one person carry a pattern that may be shared by many.
Output

What you can produce

  • PDF: preferred send-ready submission package.
  • DOCX: editable copy for residents who need to add, remove, or adjust information before sending.
  • TXT: fallback only where PDF or DOCX is not suitable.
  • Cover letter: recipient-facing explanation of why the record is being sent.
  • Recipient action summary: short navigation summary for the selected recipient.
  • Archive receipt: optional email copy for grouping records and preserving community memory.
  • Phone-safe filenames: unique file names to prevent one resident account overwriting another.
Archive

Why receipts matter

Archive receipts let accounts be grouped by issue, location, pattern, authority, and requested outcome. The archive does not replace the resident account. It helps reveal whether many separate accounts form one local issue.
