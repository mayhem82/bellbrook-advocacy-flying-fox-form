RETURN TO PORTAL

Method first, not role-play

The portal does not ask anyone to pretend to be a lawyer, regulator, investigator, doctor, scientist, or expert witness. It records the investigation method used by Julian Minko to collect accounts, separate evidence types, preserve wording, identify authority pathways, and avoid conclusions that the evidence does not support.

Investigator-led workflow

The portal is a recording and routing tool. The investigation method is investigator-led. Residents provide accounts and evidence leads. The investigator organises those accounts, checks for convergence, separates evidence from inference, and decides whether a discovery record should become a dedicated issue module.

Core rule

Every issue is handled in evidence-safe layers.

  • Resident account: what the person saw, experienced, heard, photographed, reported, or was told.
  • Evidence held: photos, videos, maps, letters, emails, dates, reference numbers, witnesses, reports or other records.
  • Inference: what the evidence may suggest, clearly marked as an inference rather than a confirmed fact.
  • Verification needed: what needs testing, inspection, disclosure, confirmation, or professional review.
  • Action sought: what the resident is asking an authority, representative, advocate, or reviewer to do.

Geospatial Canopy Health Protocol

This is an investigator-led protocol used where a local issue involves tree canopy condition, suspected dieback, repeated roost pressure, access corridors, environmental change, or visible canopy damage over time.

Google Earth, historical imagery, GPS-tagged ground photos, direction or compass-bearing photos, tilt or camera-angle notes, street-view comparisons, and near-infrared or vegetation-health imagery where available can be used together to build a time-sequenced canopy record.

  • Baseline: record the earliest available canopy condition and site context.
  • Repeat observation: compare later imagery against the baseline using the same tree, canopy group, or mapped location.
  • Ground control: attach phone photos that show GPS location, direction, tilt, date, and visible target canopy.
  • Canopy indicators: record crown thinning, leaf loss, branch dieback, canopy gaps, colour change, visible roost concentration, and damaged limbs.
  • Infrared or vegetation-health layer: where available, use near-infrared or vegetation index evidence to flag possible canopy stress earlier than normal visible imagery.
  • AI-assisted review: use AI to help compare imagery, locate repeated change zones, organise observations, and identify where follow-up photos should be taken.

Investigation use: this protocol helps the investigator decide where to take repeat photos, what trees or canopy zones to watch, whether damage appears to be expanding, and whether the pattern justifies a dedicated issue module or submission pack.

Evidence limit: geospatial and canopy imagery can show location, pattern, proximity, visible change, and possible vegetation stress. It does not prove cause, responsibility, legal breach, disease, contamination, or the exact biological mechanism without further verification.

Discovery before conclusion

The discovery module exists so locals can identify the next issue before the portal defines it. A discovery record does not decide the complaint. It gathers enough local accounts to see whether there is a repeated pattern.

How an issue becomes priority

An issue becomes a priority when archive receipts show enough convergence.

  • Volume: multiple residents submit records about the same issue.
  • Convergence: accounts point to the same location, event, authority, process, or impact.
  • Severity: safety, health, access, housing, children, elderly people, financial loss, environmental concern, or community disruption.
  • Evidence strength: photos, documents, official replies, dates, reference numbers, or witnesses.
  • Response failure: no reply, unclear reply, circular referral, delay, contradiction, or missing record.
  • Fixability: a clear responsible body, clear ask, or clear document gap.
  • Community yield: whether a dedicated module would help locals act together.

Promotion model

The discovery slot moves forward.

  • Issue 1 is a dedicated issue module.
  • Issue 2 is under construction.
  • Issue 3 begins as local discovery.
  • If discovery receipts cluster around a specific subject, that subject becomes Issue 3.
  • The discovery slot then becomes Issue 4.
  • The cycle repeats as local issues are surfaced.

What the methodology prevents

  • Calling something proven before it is verified.
  • Turning a resident account into a third-person summary without permission.
  • Claiming legal action has commenced when it has not.
  • Mixing map/location evidence with material, health, canopy, geospatial, or authority-response evidence.
  • Letting official silence erase local memory.
  • Letting one resident carry a pattern that is actually shared by many.
Output

Archive

Why receipts matter

Archive receipts let accounts be grouped by issue, location, pattern, authority and requested outcome. The archive does not replace the resident account. It helps reveal whether many separate accounts form one local issue.
